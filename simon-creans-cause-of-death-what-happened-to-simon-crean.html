In 1979, he became the general secretary of the Storemen and Packers’ Union.

His dedication and leadership propelled him to the position of vice president of the Australian Council of Trade Unions (ACTU) in 1981, ultimately becoming the president in 1985.

Crean’s political career began in 1990 when he was elected as the representative for Hotham in the House of Representatives.

Stepping down from his union responsibilities, he assumed the role of Minister for Science and Technology in the Hawke government.

Throughout his time in parliament, he held various ministerial positions until the Labor Party’s defeat in the 1996 election.

He was elected as the deputy leader, taking over from Gareth Evans.

Despite facing further defeat in the 2001 election, Crean’s resilience and determination led him to succeed Kim Beazley as the party leader and opposition leader.

Although his leadership initially generated optimism, Crean encountered challenges in public opinion polls.

In June 2003, Crean faced a leadership challenge from Beazley but emerged victorious.

Despite this, persistent speculation about his leadership prompted him to announce his resignation in November 2003, becoming the first Labor Party leader to not contest a federal election.

Mark Latham, who had been serving as his shadow treasurer, succeeded him as party leader.
